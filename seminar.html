Active Matter

Definition: systems in which individual constituents consume energy from their surroundings to generate motion and mechanical stress.

Key aspects:

  • Inherently out of equilibrium
  • Emergent self-organisation, complex and collective behaviours
  • Present across different time and length scales

Questions:

  • How do we study these systems?
  • Can our theories and techniques for equilibrium systems be extended?

Flocking and the Vicsek Model

Mermin-Wagner's Theorem [5]: A system in equilibrium cannot undergo continuous symmetry breaking in less than three dimensions.

Proof Idea:

Consider a phase with spontaneous magnetisation: \[\mathbf{s}=\left(\sqrt{1-\sum_\alpha\sigma_\alpha^2},\sigma_1,\cdots,\sigma_{N-1}\right), \quad |\sigma|\ll 1, \quad 1 \leq \alpha \leq N-1\] The reduced hamiltonian is: \[H = - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{r,r'}J(r-r')\mathbf{s}({r})\cdot \mathbf{s}({r}')\quad \xrightarrow[\text{long-wavelength fluctuation}]{\text{continuum limit}}\quad H = \text{const} + \frac{K}{2}\int \sum_\alpha (\nabla \sigma_\alpha)^2 d^dr\] The first order correction to the spontaneous magnetisation is given by: \[\langle \sigma_\alpha(r_1)\sigma_\alpha(r_2)\rangle=\frac{1}{K}\int_{BZ}\frac{e^{ik(r_1-r_2)}}{k^2}\frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^d} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \langle s_1 (r)\rangle =1-\frac{1}{2}\sum_\alpha \langle \sigma_\alpha(r)^2\rangle \sim 1- \int_{BZ}\frac{d^dk}{k^2}\] For long wavelengths, the integral two or less dimensions! The assumption of a an order phase in non-zero temperature is incorrect, hence, the system cannot undergo a continuous symmetry breaking in less than three dimensions.

Energetic argument [6]:

If we form a domain of size l by forcing the spins near the middle to point in the opposite direction of those further away: \[\begin{align} E&\sim L\frac{\pi^2}{L^2} && \implies E\sim L^{d-2} \end{align} \]

In Vicsek's model the motion of the particles gives rise to effective long-range spatial interactions among the particles in the system and hence, long-range order, producing the phase transition.

Flocking and the Vicsek Model

After Vicsek's findings, other authors came forward suggesting that the phase transition in the Vicsek model is actually first order (discontinuous).

The nature of the transition depends on the way the noise is introduced into the system:

[7]

Intrinsic/ scalar noise (○): \[\theta_i(t+\Delta t) = \langle\theta_i(t)\rangle_r + \eta\xi_i\] Extrinsic/ vectorial noise (□): \[\theta_i(t+\Delta t) = \langle\theta_i(t) + \eta e^{i\xi_i}\rangle_r\] where \[\eta \in [0,1], \quad \xi_i \in [0,2\pi]\]
In the extrinsic noise system:
  • Discontinuous drop in the order parameter.
  • There are evidences of a 1st order transition characterised by phase coexistance - Binder cumulant:

    • \[G = 1-\frac{\langle\phi^4\rangle}{3\langle\phi^2\rangle^2}\]

Flocking and the Vicsek Model

To ascertain the nature of the phase transition in the case of intrinsic noise, techniques used for equilibrium systems like finite-size scaling and short-time dynamic scaling (STD scaling) can be used [8]:

Finite-size scaling

System size is varied and the order parameter and susceptibility are fit to the scaling relations: \[ \phi(\eta,L)=L^{-\beta/\nu}\tilde{\phi}\left((\eta-\eta_c)L^{1/\nu}\right) \rightarrow \phi\sim(\eta_c-\eta)^\beta\] \[\chi(\eta,L)=L^{\gamma/\nu}\tilde{\chi}\left((\eta-\eta_c)L^{1/\nu}\right) \rightarrow \chi\sim(\eta-\eta_c)^{-\gamma}\] where, \[\eta_c(\rho)\sim \rho^\kappa,\quad \kappa\approx 1/2\]

STD scaling

Systems begins in ordered/disordered phase and is "annealed" to the critical noise. The order parameter and susceptibility are fit to the scaling relations: \[ \phi(t, \eta,L)=b^{-\beta/\nu}\bar{\phi}\left(b^{-z}t,b^\nu(\eta-\eta_c)L^{1/\nu},b^{-1}L\right) \] \[\chi(t,\eta,L)\sim b^{\gamma/\nu}\bar{\chi}\left(b^{-z}t,b^\nu(\eta-\eta_c)L^{1/\nu},b^{-1}L\right)\] where, \[\xi\sim t^{1/z} \ll L, \quad b\equiv t^{1/z} \quad \implies \quad \phi\sim t^{-\beta/{\nu z}} \quad \chi\sim t^{\gamma/{\nu z}}\]
Second order phase transition with exponents: \[ \begin{align} 0.42(4) \leq &\beta \leq 0.45(3) \\ 1.3(3) \leq &\nu \leq 1.6(3) \\ 1.87(4) \leq &\gamma \leq 2.3(4) \end{align} \] defining its own universality class.

Active Brownian Particles (ABPs)

Brownian motion is the random motion of particles suspended in a fluid caused by the collisions of the particles with the atoms of the fluid. They key characteristics are:

  • System has no memory due to the high collision rate
  • There is no preferential direction of motion
  • Linear and angular momentum are preserved
\[\partial_t\rho(r,t)=D\nabla^2\rho(r,t) \implies \text{MSD}\sim Dt\]

It can be simply modelled by the Langevin Equation:

\[\gamma\dot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla U + \mathbf{\Gamma}(t)\]

To make it active we add a force:

\[\gamma\dot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla U + \mathbf{F_a}+\mathbf{\Gamma}(t)\]

Active Brownian Particles (ABPs)

In the case of multiple particles we must include a repulsive/attractive force between the different particles.
\[ \begin{align} \gamma \mathbf{\dot{r}}_i& = F_\text{act}\mathbf{n}_i - \nabla_i \sum_{i\neq j}U(r_{ij}) + \sqrt{2\gamma k_B T}\xi_i \\ \mathbf{n}_i &= (\cos\theta_i(t),\sin\theta_i(t)) \quad \text{with} \quad \dot{\theta}=\sqrt{2D_{\theta_i}}\eta_i \quad \text{and} \quad D_\theta = 3k_BT/\gamma \sigma^2_d \end{align} \]
\[ \begin{align} &\langle\xi_i(t)\rangle=0, \quad \langle\xi_i(t)\xi_j(t^\prime)\rangle=\delta_{ij}\delta(t-t^\prime)\\ &\langle\eta_i(t)\rangle=0, \quad \langle\eta_i(t)\eta_j(t^\prime)\rangle=\delta_{ij}\delta(t-t^\prime) \end{align} \]
For the particle interaction we usually use a truncated and shifted Lennard-Jones potential:
\[ \begin{align} &U(r_{ij}) = \begin{cases} U_{LJ}(r_{ij})-U_{LJ}(r_0) && \text{for } r\leq r_0 \\ 0 && \text{for } r > r_0 \end{cases} \\ &U_{LJ}(r) = 4\epsilon\left[\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12}-\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6\right] \end{align} \]
How do these structures emerge in the parameter space of: \[ \begin{align} \phi_P = N\frac{\pi \sigma_D^2}{4S} && \text{Pe} = \frac{Lv_\text{act}}{D} = \frac{\sigma_D F\text{act}}{k_B T} \end{align}\]

Active Brownian Particles (ABPs)

[9]

General characteristics of 2D-ABP systems:

  • Above a critical packing fraction and activity, large clusters form - Motility Induced Phase-Separation (MIPS).
  • The mechanism behind MIPS:
    • particle symmetry
    • diffusion supression for higher densities
    • subdiffusive initial regime (collision time < rotational diffusion time)
  • Activity plays a role of attraction force between particles.
  • Information in the structure factor:
\[S(\mathbf{k})=\frac{1}{N}\langle\sum_{ij}e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}_{ij}}\rangle\] Activity induces order!

Active Brownian Particles (ABPs)

In 2018, Digregorio et al. completely quantified the phase-diagram of 2D ABPs.

  • Orientational order parameter and its correlation, \[ \begin{align} \psi_{6i}=\frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{j=1}^{N_i}e^{i6\theta_{ij}} && g_6(r)=\frac{\langle \psi_{6i} \psi_{6j}^*\rangle_{r_{ij}=r}}{\langle |\psi_{6i}|^2\rangle} \end{align} \]
    • of a new phase of matter: hexatic phase characterised by quasi-long-range orientational order and short-range positional one. \[ \begin{align} g_{6_\text{liquid}}(r)\sim e^{-r/21} && &g_{6_\text{hexatic}}(r)\sim r^{1/4}\sim g_{6_\text{BKT}}(r)\\ C_{{q_0}_\text{hexatic}}(r)\sim e^{-r/11} && &C_{{q_0}_\text{solid}}(r)\sim r^{1/3} \end{align} \]
  • Low activity disrupts order inducing melting.
  • High activity induces MIPS - rearrangement hexatic regions - prevailing over hexatic and solid phases.

[11] Digregorio P. et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 121, 098003 (2018)

Conclusions

  • Active systems are prevalent across different scales and disciplines.
  • The techniques used in equilibrium systems can be applied and our theory extended to active systems.
  • The analysis of these systems is more complex and new behaviours emerge.
  • It is an active and vibrant area of research with a lot left to be explored.
